UF1
Daily Asset Planning
1 of 4
Current Workflow
The core operational heartbeat. Every 2 days, the CEO and President review all active and upcoming jobs, identify equipment shortfalls, and develop a plan to cover them.
1
Open the master Excel spreadsheet (updated every 2 days)
2
Scroll through 30+ job rows reviewing equipment allocations
3
Check the bottom summary section for total deployed, total owned, available, and utilization %
4
Identify equipment types where utilization is at or above 100% (red / negative availability)
5
For each shortfall, manually search job rows for upcoming end dates — "who's coming in with some freedom?"
6
Evaluate options: convert valve sizes, pull from consignment, arrange sub-rental
7
Call DMs to communicate changes — "you need to convert this to three-inch wings"
8
Update spreadsheet cells manually with new allocations
9
Repeat every 2 days — takes several hours each session

UF2
New Job Setup & Equipment Forecasting
2 of 4
Current Workflow
When a new job is won or a frac schedule is received, the equipment requirements must be entered and checked against available assets. This determines whether Spartan can take the work.
1
Sales/operations receives a frac schedule from a customer (e.g., a 6-well pad)
2
Delmar determines what equipment is needed: type, size, pressure, quantity per well
3
Add a new row to the spreadsheet with customer name and well site
4
Fill in quantities per equipment column (free-text, inconsistent across jobs)
5
Scroll to the bottom to see if the new job pushes any equipment type into negative availability
6
If negative — begin the shortage resolution flow (see UF3)
7
Jobs beyond ~2 weeks are entered speculatively — "the future don't look good... I'm going to be short six right here"

UF3
Shortage Resolution
3 of 4
Current Workflow — "Robbing Peter to Pay Paul"
When demand exceeds supply for a specific equipment type, the CEO must find a way to fill the gap. This is the most complex and time-consuming workflow — it requires evaluating job timelines, size conversions, consignment availability, cross-district transfers, and sub-rentals.
1
See a negative number in the availability summary — "-6 on 7-1/16" 15K wing valves"
2
Scroll through all 30+ job rows looking for jobs with end dates that release that equipment type before the shortfall date
3
If none found, evaluate size conversion: "can I convert this customer from 4-inch to 3-inch wings?"
4
Call the customer to pitch the conversion — "tell them why three-inch is a better deal"
5
If still short, check consignment availability across partners (separate Excel tabs)
6
If still short, check other districts — "does Keithville have surplus?" — then verify pulling assets won't create a shortfall there
7
If still short, arrange a sub-rental from a third party
8
Update the spreadsheet with the solution — previously "black" cells (impossible) may turn green (resolved)

UF16
Job Timing Update & Conflict Check
4 of 4
Current Workflow
When a job's start or end date changes — customer delay, early release, schedule extension — the entire spreadsheet must be re-scanned to find what breaks. A single date change can take 30+ minutes on a busy week.
1
Learn a job's dates are changing (customer call, field update, weather delay)
2
Open the spreadsheet and find the job row
3
Manually adjust the date columns
4
Scroll to the bottom — check if the date change pushes any equipment type into negative availability
5
If a shortfall is created, manually scan other job rows to find which jobs are affected
6
If moving a job earlier, check whether equipment is available sooner — often it's not because other jobs still hold those assets
7
Call DMs and President to communicate the ripple effect
8
Process can take 30+ minutes for a single date change on a busy week
